PART 2

Jesse Sampson: Morning everyone, I'm Jesse Sampson, the coordinator of Middletown Urban Farming Scheme, or MUFS as we usually call it. This programme's been a real success in helping city residents grow their own fruit and vegetables, basically turning them into urban farmers if that's what they're into. You might be wondering who actually started MUFS? Well, the idea of urban farming has been around for ages, especially among town planners. But in our case, it was actually a bunch of local business owners, myself included, who decided to kick things off. Later on, some agriculture experts from the university jumped on board as well.

Now what did MUFS set out to achieve? It wasn't about cutting carbon emissions or anything like that — at least not at the start. The main aim was to make better use of empty or unused land around the city. Over time, though, we realised there was another big plus — encouraging people to eat fresh homegrown produce really helps improve their diets.

So who's taking part? Well, schools have been very active, and we've also had a good response from company employees, so the message is definitely getting through there. But next year we'll be focusing on getting community centres for senior citizens involved because, so far, their participation's been pretty low.

Now MUFS is built around a great group of gardening enthusiasts. They might not know every detail about every plot, but they really know their way around a garden. Most of them are amateurs, but they're more than happy to chat with members and share hands-on tips. Just don't expect them to answer really technical questions like the Latin names of plants — that's not their thing.

We also want to make sure people with physical disabilities can join in. We're currently working on a scheme to loan out specially adapted gardening tools so those who can't use standard equipment aren't left out. That service isn't quite ready yet, but at the moment, we do provide free window boxes and large containers. Members just need to bring their own soil and compost.

One thing I'm especially proud of is what we've done with local schools, both primary and secondary. At first, teachers thought the scheme would just be handy for science lessons. And they were right about that. It hasn't lowered school meal costs like some hoped. But there's been an unexpected bonus. Students have become much more aware of their environment, and they really want to keep public spaces clean and tidy.

Outside schools, lots of businesses and organisations have been really generous, offering free resources and services. Let me give you a few examples. The City Hospital, for instance — they prepare thousands of meals every day for patients — runs a course led by their kitchen manager. It teaches our members how to cook a wide range of dishes using vegetables.

The local government's also been very supportive. They own a big glass house that the Parks Department normally use to grow flower seedlings in winter. They've now given MUFS members free access to it so we can get our own seedlings off to a good start.

And the university? They've been fantastic too. If our members send them a soil sample, they'll run tests to figure out the best plants, fertilisers, and nutrients for that specific soil.

Oh, and to top it off, a local supermarket is offering classes on how to lose weight and stay healthy with a sensible diet.

That is the end of part two.
