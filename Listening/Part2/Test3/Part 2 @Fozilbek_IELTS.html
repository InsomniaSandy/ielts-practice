PART 2

Radio host: Hello everyone. Next week sees the return of our Town's annual International Week, an event that's been running for several years now. I'm delighted to be joined today by the organizer of this much loved celebration, Lowell Moran. Lowell, could you tell us how it all began?

Lowell: Thanks for having me. I'm really pleased to be here. Well, it all started five years ago on a cold February day. I was chatting with a few local business people about what we could do to brighten up August when the weather's decent and the children are off school until September. Someone suggested that, given how diverse our community is, we ought to hold an international event to celebrate it. The original idea was simply to invite people to share aspects of their home countries, and we all thought it was brilliant. The very first celebration was just a one day affair, featuring three countries, but it went down a storm. One of the town's biggest newspapers even covered it. The response was incredible. Loads of people wanted to get involved. So the following year, despite the higher costs, we stretched it to a full week. That's quite a progression.

Radio host: So what form does the event take these days?

Lowell: These days, the first six days each spotlight a different country, and then on the final day, weather permitting, we throw a street party near the town hall. There's live music from the local radio station, all sorts of food, and plenty of games. If it rains as it did last year, we simply move everything to the school gym. That worked perfectly well. Everyone still had a great time. Preparations kick off in January, when applications open for groups keen to host a themed day for their country. People don't need to belong to an official cultural group. Anyone who identifies with the country can send in a proper plan. The committee chooses the most creative ideas. Funding isn't a big issue at that stage. Because we do have access to grants, we also arrange a space for each group to run their activities. Sadly, we can't use the town stadium for the larger outdoor events this year, as it's already booked, but the community centre very kindly lets us use its open air car park instead. Local businesses have been hugely supportive as well. Lots of traders lend us things like tables and amplifiers free of charge for the week, and the committee deals with all the administration. The town library also makes a contribution by putting on displays of books linked to the countries we're featuring. It gives the whole celebration an extra educational angle.

Radio host: Lowell, could you tell us what forms these cultural activities usually take during the themed days?

Lowell: Each country's themed day usually includes something special, maybe a performance, an exhibition, or a demonstration. For example, people might show how to make traditional crafts or put on a photography exhibition of famous landmarks.

Radio host: That sounds fantastic. Can you give us a sneak preview of this year's highlights?

Lowell: We've got some brilliant events lined up. Monday is Mexico Day, and the group are preparing a show that promises to be as fiery as their famous chilli peppers. Expect plenty of singing, playing songs, and drumming, all of which are bound to get you moving. On Tuesday it's South Africa Day, featuring an exhibition of traditional robes and dresses worn at ceremonies and dances. Some of them are said to be worth more than their weight in gold. Wednesday will be Indonesia Day, and that's definitely one for the foodies. Two women will be showing how to prepare iconic Indonesian dishes and sauces. You'll be able to take photos at each stage so you can recreate them easily at home. Then on Thursday we've got Canada Day, moving beyond the stereotype of Canadians as only rugged outdoor types, they'll demonstrate how they carve delightful figures of animals from local legends like birds and bears out of native wood using just a knife, and Friday will be Italy day.
