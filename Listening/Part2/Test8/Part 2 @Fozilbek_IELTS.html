SECTION 2

Questions 11-14

Hi everyone, welcome to Urupukapuka Island. It's a stunning spot full of fascinating nature and history, and I'm sure you'll love it here. My name's Stellan Colby, and I'll be your guide today. Before we set off, let me give you some quick background about the island, along with a few tips for your visit.

So, first things first, Urupukapuka is actually the largest of the 140 islands that make up the Bay of Islands. Both the Maori and later European settlers lived here. The Maori were the first people to arrive long before Europeans turned up about two hundred years ago. Back then, there were around 2,000 Maori living on the island. Archaeological evidence suggests they probably settled here about one thousand years ago, and the remains they left behind give us lots of insight into their daily lives.

Now, what did they live on? Well, most of their food came from the waters around the island, which were, and still are, teeming with fish. They did try to grow potatoes, but the soil here isn't nearly as fertile as it is on the mainland. Another major difficulty was the lack of trees, which made it hard to build houses and canoes.

The island also holds great cultural and spiritual significance for the Maori, so please be respectful, especially around archaeological sites. Feel free to take photos, no problem at all. Some people think you should keep your voice down or remain silent, but honestly, that's not necessary. Just remember, eating or drinking at sacred sites is not allowed. Elsewhere on the island, though, you're welcome to enjoy your snacks and drinks.

One more important reminder: do keep an eye on the time. Once the last ferry has gone, there's no other way off the island. Normally the last one leaves at 6PM, but as today is a public holiday, the 06:00 service won't be running. The very last ferry is at 5PM sharp, so please make sure you're at the wharf by 04:50 at the latest.

Questions 15-20

Alright, let's talk about the highlights you can explore here. Take out your map. We're right at the bottom, at the wharf where the ferry dropped us off. The first stop is the Hill Fortress. Before Europeans came, Maori tribes often fought one another, so they built fortresses in strong positions, usually on hilltops or along ridgelines. Many of these were very cleverly designed and are really worth seeing. To get there, head straight from the wharf, take the second path on the right, follow the track behind Puka Bay, and the fortress is right at the tip of the headland. (Location D)

Another must-see is the Maori village. It shows how the Maori lived and gives you a real sense of their daily life. From the wharf, follow the path to the shelter, go past the lookout on your right, and the village is just a bit further on the left. (Location E)

If you'd rather not walk too far, have a look at the food storage pits. They're like cellars that kept food cool. From the wharf turn left down to Paradise Bay, then turn left again along the beach path. The pits are very close by. (Location A)

For those happy to walk a bit more, the garden terraces are fantastic. Just keep going past the shelter, then turn right at the first intersection. The terraces also give you a wonderful panoramic view of Palm Bay. (Location I)

Families with children often enjoy the Gumdiggers' Pits, though they are a bit further away. From the wharf turn left, then turn right at the first intersection and keep straight on. Don't turn left to Indigo Bay, just carry on, and you'll find them on the Northern Cliffs. (Location C)

And finally, the meeting house. It's really close by, just walk up to the shelter, turn left, and it's right there in front of you. The Maori used it for all sorts of gatherings, celebrations, funerals, and other important events. (Location G)

Okay, that's the overview of the island's main sites. If you've got any questions, just ask, I'm here to help.

That is the end of part two.
