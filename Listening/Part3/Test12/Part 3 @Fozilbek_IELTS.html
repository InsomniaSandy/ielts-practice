SECTION 3

Questions 21-25

Amy: Hi Mike, aren’t these Maori greenstone tikis we’ve been studying in class fascinating?
Mike: Oh hi Amy. They’re a bit organic for my taste, but I can see the skill involved in making them.
Amy: The stories behind them make them interesting, but the craftsmanship is what impresses me – it requires great expertise and time.
Mike: True. And there aren’t many genuine old ones around. Archaeologists rarely discover such treasures.
Amy: I believe it’s because the owners valued them. Many would have been passed down through the generations and remain in families today, like heirlooms.
Mike: Do you know what they were used for? I thought they were just decorative, but Professor Matiou says the Maori believed tikis were sacred and could be used as a pathway to their ancestors.
Amy: So not tribal symbols then? Interesting. How many different kinds are there?
Mike: Traditional tikis can be divided into two groups. It’s the way their feet, heads and hands are placed that makes the major difference – not materials or where they were made.
Amy: Is it easy to tell ancient stones from modern reproductions?
Mike: Modern ones may use a different type of stone, but the real giveaway is that they have no blemishes. Genuine carvings aren’t always perfect because the Maori used simple hand tools.

Questions 26-30

Amy: It’s amazing they produce such detailed tikis with such simple tools. Talk me through the process.
Mike: First they had to get a blank of the right size. They used a hard stone tool to wear a groove into the greenstone – that’s the scoring tool.
Amy: Right. Then they started carving?
Mike: Not straight away. Before carving, they needed an absolutely smooth, flat surface. They used a heavy sandstone block and rubbed the piece on it.
Amy: So how did they cut out the little details like hands and feet?
Mike: They had bone points for carving fine details. Small chips of bone were shaped and used like tiny chisels.
Amy: And how did they make holes, for example for the eyes?
Mike: They used a stick drill – a simple wooden shaft with a sharp tip, spun by hand.
Amy: And the coloured decorations?
Mike: Those were fixed with a special plant glue, made from tree resin, which held the inlays in place.
Amy: Fascinating. Thanks, Mike.
