SECTION 3

Questions 21-24

Emma: Hi, John. You wanted some advice about the work experience program?
John: Yeah. I wanted to check I’ve understood what I’m supposed to do.
Emma: Sure. For a start, we have to work for a company in the holidays between the first and second year, and again between the second and third year, right?
John: Yes, usually you do around fifty days with two different employers, like one hundred days in total.
Emma: I see. You know, there’s one aspect of the Work Experience Programme I’m unhappy with. I mean, we have to attend a preparation course for it, and that it’s a big investment in terms of time.
John: What’s the problem?
Emma: Just the fact that no matter how well we perform on the programme, it makes no difference whatsoever to our final grade for our degree. It goes unrecognised.
John: I know, but there are lots of advantages too. Anyway, when does your preparation course for the work experience start?
Emma: Soon. We’re starting with a session on interview technique, how to respond to questions, that stuff.
John: That can be useful. But I remember we had to make up a list of things we wanted to achieve during the time with the employer. That was a bit pointless.
Emma: My thoughts exactly. You can’t decide on your aims until you’ve started work for real. The tutor will give you some good advice about presenting your resume though, so don’t miss that session.
John: Okay. When the work experience starts for real, it would be great if I could actually do a bit of software design.
Emma: That’s highly unlikely on a work experience course. You’ll be doing more basic things than that, but they might start you off on the IT help desk that’s often where they need extra people. But then you’ll move on to work in areas that suit your field of study. I mean, whatever you focused on this year at college, you need to be able to put it into practice during work experience.
Emma: There has to be a match. And then we do work experience again after we finish our second year, right? I saw somewhere you can expect to earn between $20 to $25 per hour. I suppose that’s roughly what recent graduates get.
John: Yes, not bad. But I also read employers don’t necessarily have to pay you anything, because you’re not technically a real employee when you’re doing work experience.
Emma: Correct, but I haven’t heard of that happening to anyone.

Questions 25-30

Emma: Okay. And once I’ve decided which company I want to do my work experience with, what’s the process? Do I have to fill a form out first, saying why I’d be interested to work for the company and what kind of qualifications I have?
John: Yes, you can ask your tutor for a standard application form to use, or download one off the department’s website.
Emma: Okay, then after that do I have to sign something with the employer, like something that guarantees I’ll be given the work over a specific period of time?
John: Actually the next stage is you checking with the College that the kind of work you’re applying for is appropriate, so you’ll need to send them a brief job description.
Emma: All right. And then, if the College approves, they’ll speak to the company and get the name and email address of the managers that will be in charge of you – the contact details.
John: Okay, so after that is there some kind of interview?
Emma: No, but the company are legally responsible for you, and they don’t want you getting hurt by any equipment so they’ll set up a meeting. So we can talk about health and safety.
John: Exactly. And once you’ve started, remember to keep writing down what you had to do every day. Be detailed so you can show which skills you developed – that’s your record of work.
Emma: Right. And at what point might I receive a payment?
John: Not sure. But once it’s over, the college sends the employer a form, which they can choose to fill out or not, about how well you did and what they recommend you focus on next time – a feedback form.

That is the end of part three.
