SECTION 3

Questions 21–25

Dr. Harper: Good morning Jonathan, come in. You emailed me about your presentation.

Jonathan: Yes, Doctor Harper, it's for the product development course, but I'm a bit stuck.

Dr. Harper: Don't worry, have you narrowed it down to a particular product?

Jonathan: I've decided to focus on a type of mosquito net.

Dr. Harper: Really? What made you pick that?

Jonathan: Well, last summer I travelled across Africa with a friend who's studying medicine. We dropped by a medical centre in a region badly affected by malaria. While my friend was talking to a doctor, I got chatting with one of the administrators, and I discovered they've seen far fewer cases of malaria since these nets became widely available. That really grabbed my attention.

Dr. Harper: Fascinating! But mosquito nets aren't new, are they? I seem to remember they've been used in Japan for centuries.

Jonathan: That's right. In fact, legend has it Cleopatra used one in ancient Egypt, so that might be the earliest record. Later, they were also used in India... At first, they were made from silk, then cotton, and nowadays they're usually synthetic fibres. The real breakthrough came in the mid-twentieth century when insecticide-treated nets were developed for the military. The chemical they used back then was DDT, but it turned out to be harmful to people as well as mosquitoes.

Dr. Harper: And is that still a problem with modern nets?

Jonathan: Not at all. Thanks to new technology, the chemicals used today are completely safe for humans, even with daily use. They're also odourless — the only drawback is that people have to spray the nets regularly.

Dr. Harper: Which must be tricky in remote areas, where supplies aren't easy to get hold of.

Jonathan: Exactly. So, tell me more about the particular net you're looking at.

Jonathan: It's called Olyset, developed by a Japanese company. Unlike traditional nets, the insecticide is built into the fibres and released gradually rather than just sprayed on the surface... what we do know is that Olyset nets are tougher and much less likely to tear — they last at least five years, compared with one or two for most other nets.

(Pause for questions 26–30)

Dr. Harper: Now in your seminar, will you also set it in a wider context?

Jonathan: Yes, I'm planning to present it as a case study in product development. I'll begin with some background and explain why this innovation was necessary... I'll talk about vector-borne diseases, especially malaria... I've pulled together some statistics on global malaria cases each year... If you can also track down figures on the financial cost to affected countries...

Jonathan: After that, I'll give a quick overview of the history of mosquito nets before introducing Olyset.

Dr. Harper: Keep it short and clear. Bullet points on the key features and a simple comparison with older designs would work well.

Jonathan: Then I'll explain how Olyset nets are made.

Dr. Harper: The project's funding mainly comes from government grants and private donors... Funding is a key part of product development... over half are produced by a Tanzanian company called A to Z... Try to include a short profile of the company: how many staff they've got... But do make sure you include production figures... It's over 30,000,000.
