SECTION 3

Questions 21–25

Sebastian: Hi Camille, how's it all going?
Camille: Oh hi Sebastian. Honestly, I'm feeling a bit stressed at the moment, so I'm really glad you're here. I need to talk to you about my upcoming teaching placement. It's my very first one, and I don't quite know what to expect. Plus, it's going to be assessed as part of my degree.
Sebastian: I'm sure you'll do brilliantly. You'll be paired with an experienced teacher for mentoring, and they'll give you ongoing guidance and support throughout. Is there anything in particular you're worried about?
Camille: Yeah, it just feels like I have too much on my plate. I've got assignments from my university tutor, lesson plans to put together, and I also have to sit in on other teachers' lessons. It's a lot to juggle. That said, the practical side doesn't really worry me. I've done some volunteering before with students, so I feel confident about classroom management. And I'm pretty sociable and generally get on well with people, so that's not a major concern either.
Sebastian: I get that. I had exactly the same worries during my first placement, but it all worked out fine.
Camille: That's reassuring. I knew you'd be the best person to ask. So what should I be preparing now? Should I start drafting detailed lesson plans?
Sebastian: I wouldn't go that far, and I definitely wouldn't waste time trying to research the students beforehand. You'll have plenty of chances to get to know them once you're there. What I would do is check the dress code. It's worth finding out in advance, because expectations can vary a lot from one school to another.
Camille: Good point. I'll do that. During the placement, my supervisor will observe my lessons and give feedback afterwards. How should I approach those feedback sessions? Should I raise any issues I'm having with the students?
Sebastian: Not really. Your supervisor already knows all about the students and the challenges they face. The whole purpose of those sessions is to focus on your teaching. The best thing you can do is use the opportunity to get advice on how to improve.
Camille: Okay, that's clear. I really want to do well in my first placement. What should I be prioritising?
Sebastian: The focus changes with each placement. For example, in the final one, you'll need to show how you can put theory into practice, but the first placement is more about getting a sense of what a teacher actually does, both inside and outside the classroom. Later placements are more about refining your teaching skills.
Camille: That's helpful. I also wonder who I should turn to if I run into problems. If the issue is with my supervisor, I'm not sure I could raise it directly with them.
Sebastian: Actually, your supervisor should always be your first port of call if you have a problem. They're the best person to help. You should only go to your university tutor as a last resort.
Camille: Yeah, that makes sense. And I suppose going straight to the head teacher would be a bad idea. It might make things awkward for my supervisor.

Questions 26–30

Camille: What qualities did your supervisors have that impressed you? I think it would be good to emulate them.
Sebastian: Let me think. My first supervisor Bill Dyson was excellent. He really cared about his students and always took the time to make sure they understood difficult concepts. If anyone was still struggling, he'd find another way to explain it.
Camille: That's brilliant! What about others?
Sebastian: They all stood out in their own way. For instance, Heather Moran was fantastic at collaborating with other staff. She was always happy to listen to colleagues and share her insights with them.
Camille: That's so kind of her. I really hope I get someone who can patiently help me get started.
Sebastian: I'm sure you will. Another supervisor I had, Dan Shaw, taught me a lot about classroom management. He was approachable but firm, making it clear from day one who was in charge and setting out the rules.
Camille: I've always admired teachers like that.
Sebastian: Same here. Dawn Edwards, who supervised me last year, had exceptional professional self-awareness. She could immediately recognise what hadn't gone well in a lesson, and she had a very clear sense of what she needed to work on. I really value that kind of self-reflection.
Camille: It's such an important quality.
Sebastian: Absolutely. My most recent supervisor Elliott Smith had a really creative approach to teaching. He used stories, games, and other engaging methods to make even the driest material lively and imaginative.
Camille: That sounds really effective. I bet the students loved it!
