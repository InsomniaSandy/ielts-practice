SECTION 4

You will hear an anthropology lecturer talking about early migration to The Americas.

Scientists from several different disciplines — anthropology, archaeology, and geology — all agree that at some stage there was human migration from Asia to The Americas, but they disagree over when, how, or why these humans first came to The Americas. I'll run through some of the evidence briefly and try to explain the time frame, method, and reason for these migrations.

For a long time, most scientists had accepted that the original route into The Americas was from Northern Asia to Alaska. In the Far North America, evidence suggests that humans arrived via a land bridge. This consisted of a dry grassland plain about 2,000 kilometers wide. They think this environment would have been similar to present day tundra, with long cool winters and perpetual winds, but crucially, no ice.

Geologists estimate that the land bridge existed between seventy five thousand and fourteen thousand years ago. The theory is that these first humans were hunting animals, which they followed into what is now Alaska.

Biological evidence as well as archaeological remains have been found which support this theory. For example, anthropologists have found a high incidence of similarly shaped teeth in people in Northern Asia and in North America.

The archaeological evidence also indicates that from Alaska in North America, people began to travel far south. The problem is no one's sure yet what route people took or how humans would have been able to survive, because much of the land would have still been covered in glaciers, and it would have been very hard to find resources.

The other problem with this theory was that the evidence from Alaska only dated back 13,000 years. This was in the form of weapons used for hunting.

But evidence found at human settlements in other parts of The Americas dated further back than this. The most significant of these settlements is the Monte Verde site in Chile in South America. Evidence from the site includes biological remains which date back over 14,000 years, making Monte Verde the earliest known human settlement in The Americas.

This meant the date of the arrival of humans in Alaska had to be revised backwards by about two thousand years. Some scientists also think that the first settlers moved down in haste from North to South America on the Pacific Side, and findings at Monte Verde would seem to support this view. Although no boats have been discovered yet to support this theory, we all know that earlier people in other parts of the world had made use of this technology.

The site at Monte Verde was discovered in 1976, and excavations revealed a small settlement of 20 to 30 people living in a dozen huts along a small stream. Scientists were surprised to discover nine species of marine plants, including seaweed, material that the Monteverdeans must have brought from the sea, which is over 60 kilometers away.

However, there are still a number of prominent archaeologists who don't accept that the first humans arrived via Alaska and have put forward a theory of transoceanic migration instead. They say that at this time, the climate in Alaska would have been too inhospitable for humans to survive in, and so the first people must have come by sea.

They doubt the navigational skills of the early people for such a long voyage. People believe the theory due to there is a strong current in the Pacific Ocean.
