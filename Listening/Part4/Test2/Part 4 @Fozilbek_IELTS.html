SECTION 4

Questions 31-40

Music is a natural part of our daily lives. Whether we are at home, in the car, on public transport, or walking down the street, we are often surrounded by music. We listen to it on our phones, through speakers, or on our computers. Additionally, we are frequently immersed in music that we have not selected, such as that played in stores, on trains, or even in places like doctor’s offices, where it becomes part of our background environment. This leads to an interesting question: how do babies engage with music? A tremendous amount of research has focused on understanding how very young children respond to music. Studies have shown that babies can recall music that was played or sung to them prior to birth. My own research shows that this ability can last until a baby is 15 months old.

Research into how babies respond to music reveals that they are more sophisticated listeners than we assume. During their first year, babies perform even better than adults in some tasks, such as recognising changes that have been made in musical sequences. It suggests that we engage with music from the very start of life.

One of the most important studies in this field was carried out in 1976 by Helmut Moog. He studied 500 children, from infants to five-year-olds, and found that babies started responding to music just a few weeks after birth. By one year old, they were able to imitate the melodies of songs sung to them. Moog’s research has provided valuable insights into how young children engage with music, though less is known about the responses of two- and three-year-olds. Moog did find that when music was played, children aged two tended to move a great deal. However, by age three, this behaviour decreased and children began to use music more as a tool for interaction.

A more recent study I have conducted has used a technique known as ‘experience sampling’. Through this method, I have found that children today are exposed to an enormous amount of music. Songs and tunes designed specifically for children are the most popular, especially television themed tunes and songs on children’s websites. Nowadays, this type of music is more commonly accessed through digital devices, such as smartphones, tablets, or computers, rather than traditional radio broadcasts, as was the case in the past. Furthermore, pop music is also frequently played for children, often not by their choice, though. Many mothers and fathers use music to accompany daily routines, like having meals or driving their children around. Older siblings also play a crucial role in shaping younger children’s musical preferences.

In my study, one seven-year-old boy’s musical tastes were greatly affected by his older sister. Over time, he became a fan of rock music, despite his young age. He wasn’t making these choices independently. Rather, he was being shaped by the music that was being played in his home. This is a good example of how children’s musical preferences can be shaped by their environment and the people around them. So, why do children enjoy certain types of music? My research has found that there is no clear link between the music they enjoy and their personality at this age. However, it seems these connections might develop as children grow older. By then, their tastes in music could reflect their individual characteristics more clearly.

To conclude, I’d like to mention a few additional points. First of all, music has a strong influence on young people. One of the most important functions of music in children’s lives is a social one. Singing songs together, dancing, or debating which song to play next helps children develop socially. In my research, I’ve found that very few young children listen to music alone. Only one child in my study did so regularly. In my view, music is something that really ought to be shared. In this sense, technology has made it easier for families to enjoy music together. Internet platforms and social media give everyone access to a wealth of music and offer new ways to share musical experiences. Music is no longer just an individual activity.

Taking an interest in the music children listen to can offer parents and other family members a better understanding of what’s happening with them. Thus, it gives parents a starting point for discussing their children’s feelings. While musical preferences are personal, they are shaped by factors like family influence, exposure to different music, and the social context in which the music is heard.
