Speaker 2: The investigation of the medieval town of Deborah on the Western bank of the Nile between the modern villages was deliberately planned to gather a maximum amount of information on the domestic aspects of medieval Nubian life. The objects found and the nature of some of the buildings were more elaborate than had been anticipated. The first tribe in this area dates back ten thousand years, although some experts argue it was fifteen thousand years ago. Though further empirical evidence is needed to support these two periods, the site was abandoned and covered with windblown sand just north of the student frontier, revealing a cemetery far larger than any previously found in Nubia. Research conducted by members of the expedition from 1972 to 1973 showed that although the tombs had been plundered and burned, the fragments of pottery and optics found were numerous, varied, and of high quality. Paintings dating from the same period were discovered as well. They were found on many temple walls in Nubia, signifying the unification of Upper Egypt, represented by the lotus, with Lower Egypt, represented by the papyrus.

Modern physical anthropology goes beyond measuring indicators such as skull size or body weight. It attempts to reconstruct the entire biological structure of a population or a segment of a population. The expedition examined a total of 107 skeletons from children and women. Questions arose: where were the women buried, and why did some of the children receive the same distinctions as grown men? Several female corpses were found to have unusually large teeth among this period's skeletons. Surprisingly, a large number were found to have lost their teeth at a comparatively early age, 25 to 35. Additionally, archaeologists investigated the fingers and bones of women to find answers to these questions. A large collection of remains, representing fifteen forty six individuals, dating from the beginning to the end of the Christian period around fifteen hundred AD, was submitted for computer analysis. The age distribution shows that the Nubian population, as a rule, died at an early age. The expedition examined the tomb of an official in the Egyptian administration. We even have a clear enlargement of a photograph of his big toe, with detailed shape and fragments held in the museum. What we discovered was that culturally, there was no single Nubian people.

Moreover, the people of Nubia had met diverse environmental circumstances with a wide range of adaptive social responses. Researchers are in pursuit of the Nubian people's homeland, lost to floods, which are most likely the reason that pushed them to the edge. Dental problems were a major concern. Evidently small stones in the grain were not separated, and therefore the teeth were damaged from making bread from grain without separating them first. A diet of such bread eventually ground the teeth away, exposing their roots to decay. During the harvest, the heavier grain fell back to the threshing floor while the lighter chaff blew away. After collection, the grain was placed on the ground, ready for milling, using a stone shaped like a rolling pin with a curve to match. The grinding surface was rowed back and forth, grinding the grain into flour. On the other hand, because their food didn't have sugar, Egyptians did not have normal tooth decay.
